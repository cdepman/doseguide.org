# Sourcing gaps for DoseGuide.org: citable data and citations

**This report provides paper-level citations with exact figures for each of the five sourcing categories identified.** The most critical finding across all categories: several substances (MDMA, GHB, DXM, kratom) lack true population-based dependence transition probabilities, and the commonly cited "1 in 50,000 cocaine cardiac event" figure cannot be verified in peer-reviewed literature. Below is a substance-by-substance breakdown with every recoverable number and its full citation.

---

## 1. Dependence and addiction capture rates

### Hallucinogens (LSD, psilocybin, classical psychedelics)

The most-cited estimate remains **~4.9%** (roughly 1 in 20 users developing DSM-III-R dependence) from **Anthony JC, Warner LA, Kessler RC (1994), "Comparative epidemiology of dependence on tobacco, alcohol, controlled substances, and inhalants," *Experimental and Clinical Psychopharmacology*, 2(3), 244–268**. The companion paper is Warner LA, Kessler RC, Hughes M, Anthony JC, Nelson CB (1995), *Archives of General Psychiatry*, 52(3), 219–229, DOI: 10.1001/archpsyc.1995.03950150051010. Note that "hallucinogens" in this dataset includes PCP and potentially MDMA — not limited to classical psychedelics.

A newer, prospective-style estimate comes from **Stone AL, O'Brien MS, De La Torre A, Anthony JC (2007), "Who is becoming hallucinogen dependent soon after hallucinogen use starts?" *Drug and Alcohol Dependence*, 87(2-3), 153–163, DOI: 10.1016/j.drugalcdep.2006.08.008**. Using NHSDA 2000–2001 data (n=114,241), they found **2–3% of recent-onset hallucinogen users** developed dependence within 24 months of first use under DSM-IV criteria. This is the best available incidence-window estimate.

NESARC-III data yields a crude prevalence ratio of **~6.4%**: lifetime hallucinogen use disorder prevalence (0.60%) divided by lifetime use prevalence (9.32%), from **Shalit N, Rehm J, Engel L, et al. (2019), *Addictive Behaviors*, 89, 35–43, DOI: 10.1016/j.addbeh.2018.09.020**. This uses DSM-5 "use disorder" criteria rather than DSM-IV "dependence."

### MDMA/Ecstasy

**No population-based dependence transition probability exists for MDMA.** Anthony 1994 did not separate MDMA from "hallucinogens." The only available estimates come from clinical/convenience samples and are widely considered inflated. **Cottler LB, Womack SB, Compton WM, Ben-Abdallah A (2001), *Human Psychopharmacology*, 16(8), 599–606, DOI: 10.1002/hup.343** found **43% met DSM-IV dependence** in a convenience sample of 52 ecstasy users. **Cottler LB, Leung KS, Ben Abdallah A (2009), *Addiction*, 104(10), 1679–1690, DOI: 10.1111/j.1360-0443.2009.02649.x** found **59% lifetime MDMA dependence** among 593 experienced users (median 50 pills lifetime). Both figures are criticized for sampling bias and conflation of "come-down" effects with withdrawal symptoms (see McKetin et al. 2014, *Drug and Alcohol Dependence*, 148, 188–193, DOI: 10.1016/j.drugalcdep.2014.12.031). **Schlag AK (2020), *Drug Science, Policy and Law*, 6, DOI: 10.1177/2050324520904540** does **not** provide a specific MDMA transition number.

### Ketamine

The best available estimate is **8.55% of past-year users** meeting dependence criteria, from **Barrios KP, Connolly DJ, Ferris JA, et al. (2025), "Ketamine use in a large global sample," *Journal of Psychopharmacology*, 39(1), DOI: 10.1177/02698811241273850**, using Global Drug Survey 2018 data (n=130,761, online convenience sample). No true population-based epidemiological capture rate for ketamine exists.

### GHB

**No dependence capture rate has been published for GHB.** The extremely low population prevalence (~0.05% past-year US use per Palamar 2023, *Journal of Psychoactive Drugs*, 55(4), 481–489, DOI: 10.1080/02791072.2022.2082269) makes transition probability estimation infeasible from general surveys. Expert assessment rates GHB's dependence potential at **2.55 out of 4.0** per **van Amsterdam JGC, van Laar M, Brunt TM, van den Brink W (2012), *Regulatory Toxicology and Pharmacology*, 63(1), 7–12, DOI: 10.1016/j.yrtph.2012.03.002**. A systematic review explicitly states no transition probability is available: **Dijkstra BA, Beurmanjer H, Goudriaan AE, et al. (2021), *International Journal of Drug Policy*, 94, 103230, DOI: 10.1016/j.drugpo.2021.103230**.

### Methamphetamine

Anthony 1994 grouped methamphetamine under "stimulants" at **~11.2%** transition to dependence (DSM-III-R). No meth-specific capture rate analogous to Anthony's framework has been published. **Han B, et al. (2021), "Methamphetamine use, methamphetamine use disorder, and associated overdose deaths," *JAMA Psychiatry*, 78(12), 1329–1342, DOI: 10.1001/jamapsychiatry.2021.2588** found **~52% of past-year meth users** had DSM-IV methamphetamine use disorder using NSDUH 2015–2019 data — but this is a current-users proportion, not a first-use-to-dependence transition probability, and is inflated by selection effects (casual users drop out of the past-year user pool).

### Benzodiazepines

The best nationally representative estimate is **1.5% of all benzodiazepine users** developing a benzodiazepine use disorder (DSM-IV abuse + dependence combined), from **Blanco C, Han B, Jones CM, Johnson K, Compton WM (2018), *Journal of Clinical Psychiatry*, 79(6), 18m12174, DOI: 10.4088/JCP.18m12174**, using 2015–2016 NSDUH data (n=102,000). Anthony 1994 reported **~9.2%** for the broader "sedatives" category (DSM-III-R, includes non-benzodiazepine sedatives). Among chronic clinical users, dependence rates are far higher (40–82% depending on setting).

### DXM (Dextromethorphan)

**No population-level dependence rate exists.** The literature consists only of case series. **Xu R, Li H, Liu X, et al. (2021), *American Journal on Addictions*, 30(2), 175–178, DOI: 10.1111/ajad.13114** reported relapse rates among 28 already-dependent patients (89.29% at 1 year), but this describes relapse, not incidence. DEA and SAMHSA assessments note insufficient evidence that DXM produces a withdrawal syndrome.

### Kratom

**No population-level capture rate exists.** Among already-regular users in Malaysia (>6 months use), **>50% showed severe dependence** and 45% moderate dependence (Leeds Dependence Questionnaire) per **Singh D, Müller CP, Vicknasingam BK (2014), *Drug and Alcohol Dependence*, 139, 132–137, DOI: 10.1016/j.drugalcdep.2014.03.017** (n=293 convenience sample). In the US, **80.7% of active kratom consumers** had mild/moderate kratom use disorder by DSM-5 criteria, driven primarily by tolerance and withdrawal, per **Rogers JM, Weiss ST, Epstein DH, et al. (2024), *Drug and Alcohol Dependence*, 260, 111329, DOI: 10.1016/j.drugalcdep.2024.111329** (n=395 convenience sample). These reflect rates among established users, not the general population.

### Caffeine

The best general-population estimate is **~8% of caffeine consumers** meeting all three DSM-5-proposed Caffeine Use Disorder criteria, from **Sweeney MM, Weaver DC, Vincent KB, Arria AM, Griffiths RR (2020), *Journal of Caffeine and Adenosine Research*, 10(1), 4–11, DOI: 10.1089/caff.2019.0020** (n=1,006 US adults, online quota sample). By broader DSM-IV generic dependence criteria, **~30%** met the threshold per **Hughes JR, Oliveto AH, Liguori A, et al. (1998), *Drug and Alcohol Dependence*, 52(2), 99–107, DOI: 10.1016/S0376-8716(98)00071-8** (n=162, random-digit-dialing Vermont sample). The comprehensive review is **Meredith SE, Juliano LM, Hughes JR, Griffiths RR (2013), *Journal of Caffeine Research*, 3(3), 114–130, DOI: 10.1089/jcr.2013.0016**, estimating **9–14%** using key-criteria approaches.

## 2. Safety margins: lethal-to-effective dose ratios

### The complete Gable table

The primary source is **Gable RS (2004), "Comparison of acute lethal toxicity of commonly abused psychoactive substances," *Addiction*, 99(6), 686–696, DOI: 10.1111/j.1360-0443.2004.00744.x**. The 2006 American Scientist article (**Gable RS (2006), "The toxicity of recreational drugs," *American Scientist*, 94(3), 206–208**) adapted the same data with some substitutions. All ratios assume a healthy, 70 kg, non-tolerant adult using a single substance. A "(†)" marks estimates extrapolated primarily from animal data rather than human case reports.

| Substance | Effective dose | Lethal dose | Safety ratio |
|---|---|---|---|
| Heroin (IV) | 8 mg | 50 mg | **6** |
| GHB (oral) | 1.4–3 g | 16 g | **8** |
| Isobutyl nitrite/poppers (inhaled) | 0.2 mL | 1.5 mL | **8** |
| Alcohol (oral) | 33 g | 330 g | **10** |
| DXM (oral) | 150 mg | 1.5 g | **10** |
| Methamphetamine (oral) | 15 mg | >150 mg | **10** |
| Cocaine (intranasal) | 80 mg | 1.2 g | **15** |
| MDMA (oral) | 125 mg | 2 g | **16** |
| Codeine (oral) | 40 mg | 800 mg | **20** |
| Methadone (oral) | 5 mg | 100 mg | **20** |
| Mescaline (oral) | 350 mg | 8.4 g† | **24†** |
| Flunitrazepam (oral) | 1 mg | 30 mg | **30** |
| Ketamine (intranasal) | 70 mg | 2.7 g† | **38†** |
| Nicotine (smoked) | 1 mg | 50 mg | **50** |
| DMT (oral) | 40 mg | extrapolated† | **50†** |
| Phenobarbital (oral) | 100 mg | 5 g | **50** |
| Caffeine (oral) | 100 mg | 10 g | **100** |
| Fluoxetine (oral) | 20 mg | >2 g | **100** |
| Nitrous oxide (inhaled) | 3.5 L | >525 L | **>150** |
| LSD (oral) | 100 µg | 100 mg† | **1,000†** |
| Psilocybin (oral) | 6 mg | 6 g† | **1,000†** |
| Cannabis/THC (smoked) | 15 mg | >15 g† | **>1,000†** |

GHB, DXM, and poppers **are in Gable 2004** (though not all appear in the 2006 book chapter version, which substituted some substances).

### Updates to Gable's ratios

**Lachenmeier DW, Rehm J (2015), "Comparative risk assessment of alcohol, tobacco, cannabis and other illicit drugs using the margin of exposure approach," *Scientific Reports*, 5, 8126, DOI: 10.1038/srep08126** used a complementary Monte Carlo margin-of-exposure (MOE) method with animal LD50 data from ChemIDplus. Key findings: alcohol, nicotine, cocaine, and heroin had MOE <10 (high risk); THC had MOE >10,000 (low risk). This broadly validates Gable but uses different methodology and is not directly comparable ratio-for-ratio.

No published paper has systematically corrected Gable's specific safety ratio numbers. Gable 2004 remains the definitive source for this metric.

### Substances not in Gable: 2C-B, PCP, kratom

**2C-B**: No LD50 or safety ratio data exists in peer-reviewed literature. Clinical data shows even high doses (up to 192 mg, ~8–10× typical dose) produce only moderate toxicity, suggesting a wide margin, but no formal ratio is published.

**PCP**: No Gable-style ratio published. From animal data (mouse IV LD50/ED50 ≈ 14) and human case reports (recreational dose 1–5 mg smoked; lethal dose likely >50 mg oral), the estimated safety ratio is **~10–15**, but PCP's extreme behavioral toxicity at sub-lethal doses makes the acute lethality ratio misleading.

**Kratom/mitragynine**: Mouse oral LD50 is ~477 mg/kg per **Sabetghadam A, Ramanathan S, Sasidharan S, Mansor SM (2013), *Journal of Ethnopharmacology*, 146(3), 815–823**. ED50 for analgesia = 22 mg/kg, yielding a **therapeutic index of ~21** in mice. No formal human safety ratio has been published.

## 3. Supply purity and drug checking concordance

### Ketamine

The Italy GC/MS study is **Fregonese M, Albino A, Covino C, et al. (2021), "Drug checking as strategy for harm reduction in recreational contests," *Frontiers in Psychiatry*, 12, 596895, DOI: 10.3389/fpsyt.2021.596895**. **78% concordance** between colorimetric field test and GC/MS for ketamine across 25 samples identified as ketamine (120 total samples, five nightlife events in Umbria, Italy, 2019 data). No more recent published concordance rates with specific numbers were found from European or US drug checking services, though DIMS (Netherlands), WEDINOS (Wales), and TEDI network collect ketamine data without publishing aggregate concordance statistics in accessible summaries.

### 2C-B / Tusi / "pink cocaine"

**Tusi almost never contains 2C-B.** The most comprehensive dataset comes from **Barbaro L, Bouchard JL (2024), "What is pink cocaine?" *Journal of Medicinal Chemistry*, 67(23), 20733–20736, DOI: 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.4c02821**: across 68 DrugsData samples (2016–2024) sold as "pink cocaine" or tusi, **94% contained ketamine** and **81% contained MDMA**; 2C-B was rarely or never detected. **Palamar JJ (2023), *American Journal of Drug and Alcohol Abuse*, 49(5), 546–550, DOI: 10.1080/00952990.2023.2207716** found **94.7% contained ketamine** and **0% contained 2C-series compounds** in 19 US samples. Energy Control (Spain) reports average composition of **34% ketamine by volume, 11% MDMA**. A Miami-Dade forensic case series (**Moore DM, et al. (2025), *Journal of Forensic Sciences*, 70(3), 1208–1216, DOI: 10.1111/1556-4029.70012**) found **100% contained ketamine** across 14 powders; **0% contained 2C-B or fentanyl**.

### Cocaine: levamisole and fentanyl contamination

Levamisole prevalence has **dramatically decreased**. The DEA CY2024 Annual Cocaine Report (PRB# 2025-42) found levamisole (PTHIT) in only **~5% of exhibits**, down from **87% in 2017** and **69% in 2009** (DEA/CDC MMWR 2009). Average purity reached **88%**, the highest in a decade, with **97% of Cocaine Signature Program samples uncut**.

For fentanyl contamination, the most comprehensive source is **Lim TY, et al. (2024), *Lancet Regional Health – Americas*, DOI: 10.1016/S2667-193X(24)00225-4**: across **11.9 million NFLIS-Drug samples** (2013–2023), **2.7% of cocaine samples nationally had co-occurring fentanyl** as of 2023, with **>10% in several Northeast states** (Connecticut, Massachusetts, New Hampshire, New Jersey). Community drug checking found higher rates: **14.8% adjusted fentanyl prevalence in powder cocaine** per **Zaman T, et al. (2023), *Drug and Alcohol Dependence*, 252, 110985, DOI: 10.1016/j.drugalcdep.2023.110985** (n=718 samples from 77 harm reduction programs across 25 states). Crack cocaine had **0% fentanyl** (n=53).

### LSD

The same Fregonese 2021 Italy study found **100% concordance** for LSD, but only n=4 samples. The most informative large dataset is from the **2014 Boom Festival (Portugal)**: n=245 samples submitted as LSD, with **67.3% containing only LSD**, 0.8% LSD plus adulterants, **24.1% substituted** (11.4% DOC derivatives, 9.8% NBOMe), and 7.8% containing nothing. This was during peak NBOMe substitution; current European concordance is believed to be substantially higher. A Brazilian forensic study (Santa Catarina, 2011–2017, *Forensic Science International*, 2019) showed LSD concordance collapsed to **0.1% in 2014** due to NPS substitution, recovering to 17.6% by 2017.

### Cannabis vape cartridges

The definitive study is **Blount BC, Karwowski MP, Shields PG, et al. (2020), *New England Journal of Medicine*, 382(8), 697–705, DOI: 10.1056/NEJMoa1916433**: vitamin E acetate (VEA) was found in **94% (48/51)** of BAL fluid from EVALI patients across 16 states, and in **0% of 99 healthy controls**. Minnesota CDC data found **100% (20/20)** of THC products seized during the September 2019 outbreak peak contained VEA, compared with **0% (0/10)** from pre-outbreak 2018 seizures. FDA testing of ~225 seized THC cartridges found **47% contained VEA**, at concentrations of **31–88%** (mean ~50%). Post-outbreak, VEA use has reportedly declined due to public awareness, but no systematic post-outbreak illicit market survey has been published.

## 4. MCDA harm scores for substances not in Nutt 2010

### GHB was in Nutt 2010

**Confirmed: GHB scored 18 out of 100**, ranking 9th of 20 substances, between cannabis (20) and benzodiazepines (15). Source: **Nutt DJ, King LA, Phillips LD (2010), *The Lancet*, 376(9752), 1558–1565, DOI: 10.1016/S0140-6736(10)61462-6**.

### Substances scored in subsequent MCDA studies

**Fentanyl** has been scored in four studies. The most impactful is **Broman M, Davis AK, Armstrong S, et al. (2025), "US drug policy does not align with experts' rankings of drug harms," *Harm Reduction Journal*, 23(1), 17, DOI: 10.1186/s12954-025-01390-x**, where fentanyl scored **90 out of 100** — the highest-scoring substance across all 19 drugs assessed, exceeding methamphetamine (84), crack cocaine (83), and heroin (82). Earlier, **van Amsterdam J, Phillips L, Henderson G, et al. (2015), *Regulatory Toxicology and Pharmacology*, 73(3), 999–1004, DOI: 10.1016/j.yrtph.2015.09.014** scored injectable fentanyl at **54** in a UK opioid-specific MCDA. **Bonomo Y, et al. (2019), *Journal of Psychopharmacology*, 33(7), 759–768, DOI: 10.1177/0269881119841569** (Australian study, 22 drugs) found fentanyls scored **50 for harm-to-users** (highest of all drugs). **Crossin R, et al. (2023), *Journal of Psychopharmacology*, 37(9), 891–903, DOI: 10.1177/02698811231182012** (New Zealand, 23 drugs) rated fentanyl-based opioids high on individual harm but lower overall due to minimal NZ prevalence.

**Nitrous oxide** was scored at **6 out of 100** in **Ferreira PM, Winstock AR, Schlag AK, et al. (2022), *Drug Science, Policy and Law*, 8, DOI: 10.1177/20503245221127301**, and was rated the **least harmful drug overall** in the Crossin 2023 New Zealand study.

**DMT (as ayahuasca)** scored **5 out of 100** in the Broman 2025 US study — fourth-least harmful of 19 drugs, between LSD (4) and mushrooms (3). Pure synthetic DMT has not been separately scored.

**Poppers (alkyl nitrites)** scored **5 out of 100** (tied with mushrooms as least harmful) in Ferreira 2022.

### Substances still not scored in any MCDA

- **Kratom**: Bonnet et al. 2020 (*Frontiers in Psychiatry*, 11, 592199, DOI: 10.3389/fpsyt.2020.592199) attempted to include kratom in a German survey but **excluded it** because <60% of experts had sufficient experience to rate it. No other MCDA has scored kratom.
- **PCP**: Not included in any published MCDA study.
- **DXM**: Not included in any published MCDA study.
- **Caffeine**: Not included in any published MCDA study.
- **Tramadol**: Not confirmed in any accessible MCDA; it may appear in the van Amsterdam 2015b opioid-specific study's full table (behind paywall), but this could not be verified.

## 5. Verification of five specific claims

### "1 in 50,000 cocaine users will have a cardiac event"

**This figure cannot be verified in peer-reviewed literature.** The closest source is **Mittleman MA, Mintzer D, Maclure M, et al. (1999), "Triggering of myocardial infarction by cocaine," *Circulation*, 99(21), 2737–2741, DOI: 10.1161/01.cir.99.21.2737**, which found a **23.7-fold increase** in MI risk (95% CI: 8.5–66.3) during the 60 minutes after cocaine use. This is a *relative risk*, not an absolute per-use incidence. The "1 in 50,000" figure may be a back-calculation from baseline MI rates applied to the young cocaine-using population, but **no published paper states this number**. This claim should be reframed using the Mittleman relative risk data or removed.

### "~7% of people are CYP2D6 poor metabolizers"

**Accurate for European/Caucasian populations; misleading without ethnic qualification.** The definitive citation is **Bradford LD (2002), "CYP2D6 allele frequency in European Caucasians, Asians, Africans and their descendants," *Pharmacogenomics*, 3(2), 229–243, PMID: 11972444**. Also supported by **Ingelman-Sundberg M (2005), *Pharmacogenomics Journal*, 5(1), 6–13**, which states: "7% of Caucasians, about 1% of Orientals are classified as poor metabolizers." A large meta-analysis (>336,000 subjects) found European PM prevalence at **~6.1%**: **Gaedigk A, et al. (2017), *Genetics in Medicine*, 19(1), 69–76**. Rates by ethnicity: **~5–10% European Caucasian, ~1% East Asian, ~1.4–2% African American**.

### "4 pounds of dried mushrooms" as lethal dose

**Approximately correct.** The extrapolation derives from **Gable RS (2004), *Addiction*, 99(6), 686–696, DOI: 10.1111/j.1360-0443.2004.00744.x**, which estimated psilocybin's safety ratio at **1,000×**. The rat oral LD50 for psilocybin is **280 mg/kg**. For a 70 kg person: 280 × 70 = 19,600 mg pure psilocybin. *Psilocybe cubensis* contains ~1% psilocybin by dry weight, yielding 1,960 g ≈ **4.3 lbs**. For a 60 kg person: **~3.7 lbs**. The original animal LD50 data traces to **Cerletti A (1958)** (LD50 >250 mg/kg IV in animals). This figure is a rough extrapolation from animal data; **no confirmed human death from psilocybin alone in a healthy individual has been documented**, and the amount is physically implausible to consume.

### "2 mg fentanyl is lethal"

**Well-sourced but requires qualification.** The DEA states: "Two milligrams of fentanyl can be lethal depending on a person's body size, tolerance and past usage" (DEA Facts about Fentanyl, dea.gov). The European Union Drugs Agency (EUDA) drug profile states: "The estimated lethal dose of fentanyl in humans is 2 mg." The pharmacological basis is extrapolation from monkey IV LD50 of **0.03 mg/kg** (0.03 × 70 kg = 2.1 mg), which closely matches the 2 mg estimate. Peer-reviewed reviews include **Suzuki J, El-Haddad S (2017), *Drug and Alcohol Dependence*, 171, 107–116, DOI: 10.1016/j.drugalcdep.2016.11.033** and **Armenian P, Vo KT, Barr-Walker J, Lynch KL (2018), *Neuropharmacology*, 134(Pt A), 121–132, DOI: 10.1016/j.neuropharm.2017.10.016**. This applies strictly to **opioid-naive individuals**; tolerant users may survive doses many times higher.

### Benzos + opioids as "#1 poly-drug death combination"

**Historically accurate (2004–2016) but now debatable.** The primary citation is **Jones CM, McAninch JK (2015), "Emergency Department Visits and Overdose Deaths From Combined Use of Opioids and Benzodiazepines," *American Journal of Preventive Medicine*, 49(4), 493–501, DOI: 10.1016/j.amepre.2015.03.040**. Key finding: benzodiazepine co-involvement in opioid overdose deaths rose from **18% (2004) to 31% (2011)**. CDC MMWR data (2020) showed **92.7% of benzodiazepine-involved deaths also involved opioids**. However, since ~2017, stimulant-opioid co-involvement deaths have surged: by 2023, psychostimulant-involved deaths reached **34,855** and cocaine-involved deaths reached **29,449** (many co-involving fentanyl), while benzodiazepine-involved deaths were **10,870**. In absolute death counts, stimulant + opioid combinations likely now exceed benzo + opioid. Benzodiazepines remain the **highest-risk pharmacological co-ingestant** with opioids for respiratory depression, but the claim should be qualified with a timeframe or reframed as "one of the most dangerous" rather than definitively "#1."

## Conclusion: where the real gaps remain

Five substances — **MDMA, GHB, DXM, kratom, and 2C-B** — represent the most intractable sourcing gaps across every category. None has a population-based dependence transition probability, and 2C-B lacks even basic LD50 data. For the MCDA harm framework, **kratom, PCP, DXM, caffeine, and tramadol** remain entirely unscored. The cocaine cardiac claim should be retired or reformulated around the Mittleman relative risk, and the benzos + opioids claim needs temporal qualification given the post-2017 stimulant-opioid surge. Conversely, several previously assumed gaps turn out to be well-sourced: GHB and DXM are both in Gable 2004 (safety ratios 8 and 10 respectively), GHB is in Nutt 2010 (score 18), and fentanyl now has robust MCDA scoring across four international studies, with the Broman 2025 US study providing the most policy-relevant score of 90/100. The levamisole picture has also shifted dramatically — DEA 2024 data shows prevalence has fallen from 87% to ~5%, a finding that warrants updating on any harm reduction resource still citing the older peak figures.
